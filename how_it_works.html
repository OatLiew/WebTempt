HOW IT WORKS

This project aims to develop the technology necessary to build an energy-efficient, electronic air filtration unit, which will be capable of directly replacing the existing fiber mesh filters used in large commercial buildings.

To be developed is the filter’s innovative dust collection stage, which relies on the use of a novel open-cell, electrically conductive polymer foam to capture and immobilize dust particles. This electrostatic dust capture mechanism allows the foam particle collector electrodes to be positioned parallel to the airflow, which significantly reduces the backpressure that the filter exhibits on the ventilation system’s air fan. Using this technology, the air fan’s energy usage can be reduced by up to 50% - a significant portion of the total energy usage of a commercial building.

This projects targets topic CT.7 “Sustainable Technologies for Energy Efficiency, Capture, Storage and Use.” The technology described here has the potential to significantly reduce the energy usage of large commercial buildings, while providing a substantial financial incentive for wide adoption. The filter is being developed specifically to be compatible with older HVAC installations, which are currently using highly inefficient air filtration systems, thus maximizing the potential for energy savings.

While certain alternative dust filtration technologies have made inroads in the new construction commercial air filtration market, they have been demonstrated to not be commercially viable in the vastly larger building retrofit market. Our technology addresses the downsides of such existing filtration products, namely prohibitively high costs of installation and maintenance, and enables the creation of a device that can be installed directly in place of existing fiber mesh filters, requiring minimal installation and upkeep expenditures. Being able to access the retrofit market allows this technology to make a greater global environmental impact by significantly reducing the energy usage of all types of commercial buildings, regardless of age or existing energy conserving features.
